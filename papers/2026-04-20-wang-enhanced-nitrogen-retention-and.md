## What they found
Co-composting of high-nitrogen chicken manure (CM) with keratin-rich waste wool (WW) under low C/N ratio conditions often suffers from excessive nitrogen losses due to rapid mineralization and poor keratin degradability, resulting in prolonged maturation. This study systematically evaluated the individual and combined effects of lignite (LH) and phosphogypsum (PG) on CM-WW co-composting to enhance compost quality, keratin degradation, and nitrogen retention. Four treatments were established: control (CK), LH addition (F), PG addition (P), and combined LH&#xa0;+&#xa0;PG addition (FP). Throughout the composting process, physicochemical properties, nitrogen transformation, humification indices, microbial community dynamics (via 16S rRNA sequencing), and predicted metabolic functions were monitored. The agronomic value was assessed using a pak choi (Brassica rapa subsp. chinensis) pot experiment. Results demonstrated that FP co-application significantly reduced total nitrogen loss by 55.5%, accelerated keratin degradation, and increased the humification degree (HA/FA ratio) by 14.2%. Microbial analysis revealed that FP treatment significantly altered the community structure, enhancing the co-dominance and functional synergy between Firmicutes and Actinobacteria, while predicted metabolic profiling suggested enhancements in amino acid and carbohydrate metabolism pathways. The final compost products met national safety standards for heavy metals and pathogens. Application of FP compost increased soil organic matter by 29.8% and pak choi yield by 205.6%, alongside improved nutrient availability. In conclusion, the synergistic addition of LH and PG improved composting performance for low C/N, protein-rich wastes, enhancing nutrient retention and humification, which contributes to resource recycling and sustainable agriculture.
